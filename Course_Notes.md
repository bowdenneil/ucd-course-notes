# UNIT 1: AI Transformation Readiness

## Core Idea
Assess organisational preparedness BEFORE investing in AI. Most AI failures are organisational, not technical.

## The 6-Dimension AI Readiness Framework

| Dimension | Key Question | Indicators |
|-----------|--------------|------------|
| Technology Infrastructure | Can our tech stack support AI? | Cloud maturity, API availability, compute capacity, integration architecture |
| Data Maturity | Is our data AI-ready? | Data quality, accessibility, governance, volume, integration |
| Organisational Culture | Will people embrace AI? | Innovation appetite, risk tolerance, change readiness, data-driven decision-making |
| Leadership Alignment | Is leadership committed? | Executive sponsorship, strategic clarity, investment commitment, patience for results |
| Skills & Capabilities | Do we have the talent? | Technical skills, domain expertise, data literacy, change management capability |
| Governance Readiness | Do we have rules for AI? | AI policies, risk frameworks, ethics guidelines, regulatory awareness |

Score each 1-5, create a radar chart, lowest scores are priority gaps.

## Stakeholder Mapping

- **Power-Interest Matrix:** Classify stakeholders by power over and interest in AI initiatives
- **Engagement Strategies:**
  - High Power / High Interest → Manage Closely
  - High Power / Low Interest → Keep Satisfied
  - Low Power / High Interest → Keep Informed
  - Low Power / Low Interest → Monitor

## Action Planning

- **Impact-Effort Matrix:** Prioritise actions by expected impact vs. required effort
- **Quick Wins:** High impact, low effort, visible results within 30-90 days, builds momentum
- **Strategic Initiatives:** Higher effort but essential for long-term success

## Case Study: DBS Bank — "Damn Bloody Slow" to World's Best Bank

| Phase | Period | Focus | Key Results |
|-------|--------|-------|-------------|
| Phase 1 | 2009-2014 | Foundational process improvement | 250M customer hours eliminated |
| Phase 2 | 2014-2018 | Digital transformation | Digital-first banking platform |
| Phase 3 | 2018-Present | AI scaling | 1,500+ AI models, 370+ use cases, SGD 1B+ economic impact |

**Key Insight:** AI success requires sequential investment: process improvement BEFORE digital, digital BEFORE AI. CEO Piyash Gupta led the cultural transformation.

## Workbook Exercises
- Part A: Score 6 dimensions (1-5), create radar chart, identify priority gaps
- Part B: Map top 5 stakeholders on power-interest matrix, define engagement strategies
- Part C: Use impact-effort matrix, identify 3 quick wins + 3 strategic initiatives, set 30/60/90-day milestones

# UNIT 3: AI Governance Framework Design

## Core Idea
Governance is a competitive advantage, not a compliance burden. The gap between policy documents and working governance is where most frameworks fail. Risk-proportionate governance is key.

## Six Building Blocks of AI Governance

| Component | Question | Key Elements |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| 1. Oversight Structure | Who is accountable for AI? | Board/Committee -> AI Steering Committee -> AI Governance Office -> Project/Product Leads |
| 2. Decision Rights | Who decides what? | RACI matrix for AI decisions |
| 3. Approval Process | How does an AI project go from idea to deployment? | Stage-gate with risk-proportionate review |
| 4. Risk Classification | What drives governance intensity? | Prohibited / High-Risk / Medium-Risk / Low-Risk |
| 5. Monitoring & Audit | How do we ensure ongoing compliance? | Continuous monitoring + periodic audits |
| 6. Policy & Standards | What are the documented rules? | Principles -> Policies -> Standards -> Guidelines |

## Oversight Structure — Four Levels

| Level | Body | Role |
|-------|------|------|
| Board | Board/Committee | Strategic oversight, major decisions |
| Executive | AI Steering Committee | Policy, cross-functional coordination |
| Management | AI Governance Office | Day-to-day governance operations |
| Operational | Project/Product Leads | Implementation, compliance |

## Risk Classification Framework

| Risk Level | Characteristics | Governance Approach | Timeline |
|------------|----------------|--------------------|---------|
| Prohibited | Unacceptable harm potential | Not permitted | — |
| High-Risk | Significant impact on rights, safety | Full governance, external audit | Months |
| Medium-Risk | Moderate impact, recoverable | Standard governance, internal review | Weeks |
| Low-Risk | Minimal impact, well-understood | Light-touch, self-certification | Days |

### Fast-Track Governance (Pre-Approved Pathways)
- Internal productivity tools using approved GenAI
- Low-risk analytics (descriptive dashboards, non-personal data)
- Pre-vetted vendor solutions with standard configurations

## EU AI Act — Critical Details

| Risk Category | Requirements | Maximum Penalty |
|---------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited) | Not permitted | EUR 35M or 7% global turnover |
| High-Risk | Strict requirements and oversight | EUR 15M or 3% global turnover |
| Limited Risk | Transparency obligations | EUR 7.5M or 1% global turnover |
| Minimal Risk | Light-touch regulation | N/A |

**EU AI Act Timeline:**
- Aug 2024: Entry into force
- Feb 2025: Prohibited AI practices become illegal
- Aug 2025: General-Purpose AI model obligations apply
- Aug 2026: Full implementation of high-risk system requirements
- Aug 2027: Full enforcement for embedded AI

## Operationalisation Design Principles

1. **Start with User Experience** — Who needs governance, what are their pain points?
2. **Design for the 80%** — Streamline common, low-risk cases
3. **Automate Where Possible** — Embed governance in existing tools
4. **Make the Right Thing Easy** — Clear guidance, visible pathways, quick feedback

## Governance Embedded in AI Lifecycle

| Stage | Purpose | Governance Touch |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Design | Early risk identification | Light — self-assessment with escalation |
| Develop | Technical and ethical review | Medium — review by relevant experts |
| Deploy | Pre-launch approval | Risk-proportionate — heavier for high-risk |
| Operate | Ongoing monitoring | Automated monitoring + periodic human review |
| Retire | Responsible decommissioning | Documented process, approval for data decisions |

## Case Study: Microsoft's Responsible AI Governance Journey

**Starting Point:** March 2016 — Tay chatbot catastrophe (racist/sexist outputs within 16 hours)

| Phase | Period | Key Developments |
|-------|--------|-----------------|
| Foundation | 2017-2019 | Aether Committee (2017); Six AI Principles (2018); Office of Responsible AI (2019) |
| Operationalisation | 2019-2022 | RA Standard v1; Sensitive Uses Review Programme (200+ reviews); RA Champs network; 145K+ employees trained; RA Standard v2 (2022) |
| Scaling for GenAI | 2023-Present | Sydney/Bing incident (Feb 2023); Enhanced governance for generative AI; 30+ tools with 155+ features |

**Six AI Principles:** Fairness, Reliability & Safety, Privacy & Security, Inclusiveness, Transparency, Accountability

**Key Insight:** Governance failures are the most instructive. Building comprehensive governance requires multi-year, multi-layered investment.

2. Decision Rights Matrix (RACI for 9 key AI decisions)

4. Approval Workflow (4-stage gate process with fast-track)

# UNIT 4: Technology Selection and Vendor Evaluation

## Core Idea
Choose the right tech stack and vendor through systematic evaluation, not vendor hype. Hybrid approaches usually win.

## The AI Technology Stack (5 Layers)

| Layer | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| AI-Enabled Applications | Vertical SaaS, enterprise tools | Salesforce Einstein, MS Copilot |
| AI Platforms & Tools | MLOps, model management | Databricks, DataRobot, H2O.ai |
| Specialised AI Services | Vision, speech, NLP | Clarifai, Deepgram, ABBYY |
| Foundation Models | GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama | OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta |
| Infrastructure Layer | Cloud providers, GPU clusters | AWS, Azure, GCP, NVIDIA |

## Build vs. Buy Decision Matrix

| | Low Strategic Importance | High Strategic Importance |
|---|---|---|
| **High Differentiation Potential** | BUILD or PARTNER | BUILD (with partners) |
| **Low Differentiation Potential** | BUY (commoditised) | BUY or PARTNER |

### Six Critical Evaluation Factors

1. Strategic Importance — How central to competitive advantage?
2. Differentiation Potential — Can custom development create unique value?
3. Capability Maturity — Do we have the talent to build and maintain?
4. Resource Availability — Budget for development vs. subscription
5. Time to Market — Competitive pressure for speed
6. Total Cost of Ownership — 3-5 year cost comparison including hidden costs

## Hybrid Approaches (4 Patterns)

1. **Buy Foundation, Build Differentiation:** Commercial LLMs as base, custom applications on top
2. **Buy Commodity, Build Core:** Purchase AI for support functions, build for core business
3. **Buy Platform, Build Applications:** Purchase MLOps platform, develop custom models
4. **Partner for Speed, Build for Control:** Partner initially, build internal capability over time

## Vendor Evaluation Framework (8 Criteria)

| Criteria | Typical Weight | Key Sub-Criteria |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Functional Capabilities | 20% | Core features, model quality, customisation, accuracy |
| Technical Architecture | 15% | Deployment options, API design, latency, reliability |
| Security & Compliance | 15% | Data privacy, certifications, AI Act compliance |
| Integration Capabilities | 15% | Pre-built connectors, API flexibility, SSO |
| Scalability & Performance | 10% | Throughput, concurrent users, cost at scale |
| Vendor Stability & Roadmap | 10% | Financial health, R&D investment, exit strategy |
| Pricing & Licensing | 10% | Pricing model, predictability, hidden costs |
| Support & Services | 5% | Support tiers, training, documentation |

**Weighted Scoring by Context:**
- High-regulated orgs: Security & Compliance weighted 25%
- Fast-to-Market orgs: Functional Capabilities 25%, Integration 20%

## Integration Architecture Patterns

| Pattern | Use Case | Pros | Cons |
|---------|----------|------|------|
| API Integration | Simple augmentation | Quick, low overhead | External dependency, data leaves environment |
| Embedding/RAG | Ground AI in proprietary data | Contextual responses, reduced hallucination | Additional infrastructure |
| Orchestration Layer | Complex workflows, multi-model | Flexibility, vendor optionality | Higher complexity |
| Self-Hosted/Edge | Data sovereignty, offline needs | Full control, no dependency | Infrastructure burden |

## Vendor Lock-In Types and Mitigation

| Lock-In Type | Risk Level | Mitigation |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Data Lock-In | High | Data portability in standard formats |
| Model Lock-In | High | Abstraction layers for model switching |
| API Lock-In | Medium | Build adapter interfaces |
| Integration Lock-In | Medium | Favour open standards |
| Skills Lock-In | Low-Medium | Multi-vendor POCs |
| Contract Lock-In | Medium | Negotiate data portability and exit clauses |

## Case Study: Morgan Stanley's Generative AI Platform

- **Challenge:** 350,000+ research documents, 30+ minutes per query, 16,000+ financial advisors
- **Selection:** OpenAI GPT-4 via strategic partnership (not simple licensing)
- **Architecture:** RAG with source attribution, human-in-the-loop, Salesforce CRM integration
- **Results:** 98% advisor adoption; 500K+ monthly prompts; research from 30+ mins to seconds; 80% document retrieval efficiency (up from 20%)
- **Key Insight:** Hybrid approach — buy foundation (GPT-4), build custom applications on top. Start with internal use cases to reduce risk.

# UNIT 5: Data Strategy and Infrastructure

## Core Idea
AI quality is bounded by data quality. The most sophisticated algorithm cannot overcome poor data. "The Data Iceberg" — executives see dashboards, AI needs clean integrated data underneath.

## Common Data Problems That Kill AI Projects

1. **Siloed Data:** Trapped in departmental systems, no unified view
2. **Poor Data Quality:** Missing values, inconsistent formats, duplicates, stale information
3. **Insufficient Volume:** Not enough historical data, rare events underrepresented
4. **Bias in Historical Data:** Past decisions embedded, demographic imbalances, feedback loops
5. **Accessibility Issues:** No API access, manual extraction, no real-time data
6. **Data Technical Debt:** Undocumented transformations, legacy dependencies, no lineage tracking

## The Data Readiness Framework (5 Dimensions)

| Dimension | Key Question | Scoring (1-5) |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Availability | Do we have it? | 1=Critical Gap -> 5=AI-Ready (fully available) |
| Quality | Is it good? | 6 sub-dimensions: Accuracy, Completeness, Consistency, Timeliness, Validity, Uniqueness |
| Accessibility | Can we get it? | Barriers: System silos, security, format issues, manual processes |
| Volume | Is it enough? | Classification 1,000+/class; Time series 2+ cycles; NLP 10,000+ examples; CV 1,000+/class |
| Governance | Do we manage it properly? | 5 levels: Ad hoc -> Developing -> Defined -> Managed -> Optimised |

## Data Ownership Model (3 Roles)

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| Data Owner | Business executive accountable for data domain; approves policies and access |
| Data Steward | Day-to-day management; implements quality rules and monitoring |
| Data Custodian | Technical management of data systems; implements security controls |

## Data Quality Standards for AI

| Criticality | Accuracy | Completeness | Timeliness |
|------------|----------|-------------|------------|
| Critical (Model input) | >99% | >99% | Real-time |
| High (Important feature) | >97% | >95% | <1 hour |
| Medium (Supporting data) | >95% | >90% | <24 hours |
| Low (Nice to have) | >90% | >80% | <1 week |

## Access Control Tiers

| Tier | Classification | Access | Examples |
|------|---------------|--------|----------|
| Tier 1 | Public Data | Available to all employees | Policy documents |
| Tier 2 | Internal Data | Business justification + manager approval | Ops KPIs |
| Tier 3 | Confidential Data | Data owner approval, purpose-limited, audit logging | Customer transactions |
| Tier 4 | Restricted (PII, Financial) | Executive approval, legal/compliance review, enhanced monitoring | PII/financial identifiers |

## Privacy-Preserving AI Techniques

- **Anonymisation:** Remove direct identifiers
- **Pseudonymisation:** Replace with tokens
- **Differential Privacy:** Add noise to protect individuals
- **Federated Learning:** Train without centralising data
- **Synthetic Data:** Generate representative fake data

## AI Data Infrastructure Stack (6 Layers)

| Layer | Components |
|-------|-----------|
| AI Applications | Predictions, recommendations, automation |
| MLOps Platform | Model registry, experiment tracking, deployment |
| Feature Store | Feature engineering, storage, serving |
| Data Processing Layer | Batch processing, stream processing, ETL |
| Storage Layer | Data lake, data warehouse, object storage |
| Data Sources | Databases, APIs, files, streams, external data |

## Storage Architecture Comparison

| Aspect | Data Lake | Data Warehouse | Data Lakehouse |
|--------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Approach | Schema-on-read | Schema-on-write | Best of both |
| Data Types | All (raw, native) | Structured, curated | All with ACID transactions |
| Best For | Exploratory analysis, data science | BI, reporting | Unified batch + streaming |
| Modern Choice | | | Bronze (raw) -> Silver (cleaned) -> Gold (curated) |

## Case Study: Capital One's Data Strategy and Cloud Transformation

- **Starting Point (2012):** 8 physical data centres, legacy infrastructure, siloed data, batch processing
- **Decision:** "All in" on public cloud (AWS)
- **Migration:** 8 data centres closed, ~2,000 apps migrated, 80% rebuilt cloud-native, 50+ PB data
- **Results:** Infrastructure provisioning from months to minutes; release frequency from quarterly to multiple times daily; 300% increase in app changes; 50% reduction in transaction errors

**5 Key Lessons:**
1. "Do the hard part first" — establish governance before scaling
2. Governance enables innovation, not just compliance
3. Balance scale with control (avoid going from "data starved to data drunk")
4. CI/CD is critical to scaling
5. Rebuild, don't just migrate (80% rebuilt cloud-native)

# UNIT 6: Implementation Roadmap Development

## Core Idea
"Big bang" AI deployment fails. Balance "move fast and break things" against "careful planning paralysis" with strategic phased rollout.

## Three Common Phasing Approaches

| Approach | Structure | Best For |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| Pilot -> Scale -> Expand | 3-6mo single use case -> 6-12mo multiple -> 12-24mo enterprise-wide | High uncertainty, limited AI experience |
| Sequential by Function | HR -> Finance -> Operations -> Sales (one at a time) | Function-specific solutions, decentralised orgs |
| Parallel by Use Case | Multiple use cases in parallel through same phases | Independent use cases with shared infrastructure |

## Phase Gate Decision Framework

| Criteria | GO | CONDITIONAL GO | PIVOT | NO-GO |
|----------|-----|----------------|-------|-------|
| Technical Objectives | >90% achieved | 70-90% achieved | <70% but learnings valuable | Fundamental blockers |
| Business Value | Exceeds projections | Meets projections | Below but improvable | No viable path |
| User Adoption | Strong acceptance | Moderate acceptance | Resistance addressable | Active rejection |
| Risk Profile | Within tolerance | Manageable increases | Requires mitigation | Unacceptable |
| Resource Consumption | At or below budget | Slight overrun | Significant overrun | Unsustainable |

## SMART-M Framework for AI Milestones

| Attribute | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Specific | Clear, unambiguous deliverable | "Customer service chatbot deployed" |
| Measurable | Quantifiable success criteria | "Handling 500+ queries/day with 85% resolution" |
| Achievable | Realistic given resources/constraints | Within team capacity and budget |
| Relevant | Tied to business objectives | Supports "reduce support costs 20%" goal |
| Time-bound | Clear deadline | "Complete by end of Q2" |
| Meaningful | Represents genuine progress | Not just activity, but value creation |

## Three Types of Milestones

| Type | Focus | Examples |
|------|-------|----------|
| Technical | System functionality, performance | Infrastructure deployed, model accuracy achieved |
| Business | Value delivery, outcomes | ROI targets achieved, process efficiency gains |
| Organisational | Adoption, capability | Training completed, adoption rates achieved |

## Six Resource Pillars

1. **Technology/Platform** — Cloud infrastructure, software licensing, development tools
2. **Development Resources** — Dev environment, testing infrastructure, CI/CD pipelines
3. **Data Science/AI Talent** — Data scientists, ML engineers, data engineers, AI architects
4. **Change Management & Training** — Change managers, training developers, communications specialists
5. **Business/Functional SMEs** — Process owners, domain experts, compliance specialists
6. **Project Management** — Project managers, scrum masters, business analysts, QA

## Budget Aggregation

| Category | Typical % | Key Components |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| Personnel | 50-60% | Internal staff, contractors, consultants |
| Technology | 25-35% | Cloud, software, infrastructure |
| Training | 5-10% | Materials, delivery, lost productivity |
| Contingency | 10-15% | Risk buffer, scope changes |

**Sample Budget:** Pilot EUR 770K -> Scale EUR 1.6M -> Expand EUR 2.1M = Total EUR 4.5M

## Dependency Management (4 Categories)

1. **Technical:** Infrastructure, system integrations, data pipeline completion, security approvals
2. **Data:** Access permissions, quality remediation, migration completion, governance approval
3. **Organisational:** Stakeholder availability, training completion, change readiness, policy approvals
4. **External:** Vendor deliverables, regulatory approvals, third-party integrations, market conditions

## Risk Assessment Matrix (Probability x Impact)

| | Low Prob | Medium Prob | High Prob | Very High Prob |
|---|---------|------------|-----------|---------------|
| Very High Impact | MEDIUM | HIGH | CRITICAL | CRITICAL |
| High Impact | LOW | MEDIUM | HIGH | CRITICAL |
| Medium Impact | LOW | LOW | MEDIUM | HIGH |
| Low Impact | LOW | LOW | LOW | MEDIUM |

## Four Risk Response Types

1. **Avoid:** Eliminate the risk by removing its cause (choose proven technology over cutting-edge)
2. **Transfer:** Shift risk to another party (vendor SLA commitments, insurance)
3. **Mitigate:** Reduce probability or impact (cross-train team members)
4. **Accept:** Acknowledge and prepare (accept some user resistance; plan for longer adoption curve)

## Case Study: Shell's AI Predictive Maintenance

| Phase | Period | Key Activities | Milestones |
|-------|--------|---------------|------------|
| Foundation | 2013-2018 | CoE establishment, skills building | 3,000+ community members |
| Platform Selection | 2018-2019 | Evaluated build vs. buy; selected C3 AI on Azure | 52 critical valves monitored |
| Validation | 2019-2020 | Partnership formalisation (Baker Hughes, C3 AI, Microsoft) | 23 operating assets |
| Accelerated Scale | 2021 | Open AI Energy Initiative launch | 5,200+ equipment pieces |
| Global Deployment | 2022 | Achievement of 10,000 equipment target | 10,000+ pieces, 11,000 models |

**Business Impact:** 20% reduction in unplanned downtime, 15% maintenance cost reduction, GBP 1M+ annual savings per major site

**6 Key Lessons:**
1. Foundation before scale (5 years of foundational work before enterprise deployment)
2. Clear milestone definition creates accountability
3. Platform enables scale — "It's not that hard to build ML models. What's hard is putting 2M models in production"
4. Ecosystem collaboration accelerates progress
5. Balance innovation and operational support
6. Workforce transformation is non-negotiable

# UNIT 7: Change Management and Organisational Design

## Core Idea
AI change is continuous, not episodic. Models learn and evolve post-deployment. AI affects knowledge workers and decision-makers, not just operational staff. Resistance to AI is often rational and information-based.

## Kotter's 8-Step Model (Applied to AI)

1. **Create urgency** — Quantify the cost of inaction
2. **Build a guiding coalition** — Cross-functional, including sceptics
3. **Form a strategic vision** — Connect AI to business outcomes
4. **Enlist a volunteer army** — 10% of organisation as AI champions
5. **Enable action by removing barriers** — Self-service tools with guardrails
6. **Generate short-term wins** — Visible value within 90 days
7. **Sustain acceleration** — Each win funds the next initiative
8. **Institute change** — Embed AI in processes, job descriptions, KPIs

## Resistance Pattern Analysis

| Resistance Type | Root Cause | Response Strategy |
|----------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Fear-based | Job displacement anxiety | Transparency, reskilling investment |
| Skill-based | Inability to use new tools | Role-based training, peer support |
| Value-based | Disagreement with the approach | Evidence-based dialogue, pilot results |
| Power-based | Threat to status or autonomy | Involve in design, new influence roles |

## 10 Change Management Worksheets

1. Stakeholder Mapping (power/interest matrix)
2. Resistance Patterns Analysis
3. Communication Plan (audience, message, channel, frequency)
4. Training Plan (role-based learning paths)
5. Leadership Action Plan
6. Quick Wins Identification (90-day horizon)
7. Organisational Structure Assessment
8. Metrics and Feedback Loops
9. Detailed Metrics Dashboard
10. Six-Month Action Plan

## Case Study: Microsoft's Internal AI Transformation (Copilot)

- **Scale:** 10,000 AI champions deployed across the organisation
- **Approach:** Phased rollout with governance as enabler (not blocker)
- **Self-service with guardrails model:** Employees can build AI solutions within governed boundaries
- **Six key lessons:**
  1. Start with governance — it enables rather than restricts
  2. Champions are force multipliers
  3. Peer influence outpaces policy
  4. Localisation matters
  5. Measure beyond usage (behavioural change, not just adoption)
  6. AI change is continuous, not a one-time event

# UNIT 8: Workforce Transformation and Skills Development

## Core Idea
Different roles need different skill depths — avoid "train everyone on everything." Build a skills pyramid with deep specialists supporting broad fluency. Measure behaviour change, not just training completion.

## The AI Skills Landscape

```
        AI Research (Specialists)
       AI Engineering (Technical)
      AI Application (Business Users)
     AI Fluency (All Staff)
```

## Skills Gap Analysis (Three-Step Process)

1. **Current State Assessment:** Map existing skills across the organisation
2. **Future State Definition:** Define required capabilities based on AI strategy
3. **Gap Identification:** Prioritise based on strategic importance and urgency

## Build-Buy-Borrow Strategy

| Strategy | Best For | Key Consideration |
|----------|----------|-------------------|
| Build | Core capabilities, long-term competitive advantage | Internal training, career development, highest investment |
| Buy | Leadership/specialist roles, urgent capability needs | External hiring, competitive compensation, knowledge transfer |
| Borrow | Flexibility, specific projects, knowledge transfer | Contractors, consultants, secondments — include knowledge transfer |

## Training Programme Design

- **Role-based learning paths** (not one-size-fits-all)
- **Blended delivery approaches** (online, in-person, on-the-job)
- **Measure behaviour change**, not just completion rates
- **Four training levels:** AI Fluency (all staff) -> AI Application (business users) -> AI Engineering (technical) -> AI Research (specialists)

## Case Study: AT&T "Future Ready" Programme

- **Context:** $1B investment to retrain 100,000 of 280,000 employees (started 2013)
- **Trigger:** Mobile data up 250,000%; legacy 40-year-old switches; only 50% had STEM skills vs 95% needed by 2020
- **Build-Buy-Borrow:**
  - Build: Internal training programmes, career development
  - Buy: External hiring for specialist roles
  - Borrow: Partnerships with Georgia Tech (online degrees), O'Reilly learning platform
- **Key Lessons:** Massive reskilling is possible but requires sustained investment; partnership with educational institutions is critical; must address cultural resistance to self-directed learning

# UNIT 9: Ethics, Trust, and Responsible AI

## Core Idea
Ethics is strategic — responsible AI is competitive advantage, not just risk mitigation. Companies with strong AI ethics programs show 15-20% higher customer trust scores and 30% faster regulatory approval. Principles alone are insufficient — ethics must be operationalised.

## Six Core Ethical Challenges

## Ethical Framework — Seven Essential Elements

1. **Core Principles and Values** — Foundational commitments aligned with organisational values
2. **Ethical Guidelines by AI Use Case** — Risk-based requirements and restrictions
3. **Decision Protocols for Ethical Dilemmas** — Structured approach for gray areas
4. **Review and Approval Processes** — Governance checkpoints in AI lifecycle
5. **Stakeholder Engagement Mechanisms** — Channels for input from affected parties
6. **Continuous Monitoring Systems** — Ongoing oversight of deployed systems
7. **Accountability Structures** — Clear ownership and consequences for violations

## Ethical Decision Framework (5 Steps)

1. **IDENTIFY:** What ethical issues are present? Who could be harmed? What values are in tension?
2. **ANALYZE:** What are our obligations to each stakeholder? What do our principles say?
3. **EVALUATE OPTIONS:** What alternatives exist? What are the consequences of each?
4. **DECIDE AND DOCUMENT:** Make clear decision with rationale; document trade-offs
5. **REVIEW AND LEARN:** Monitor outcomes; update guidelines if needed

## Bias Detection and Mitigation

### Sources of Bias
1. Training Data Bias (historical, measurement, label bias)
2. Algorithm Design Bias (objective function, feature selection)
3. Deployment Context Bias (population shift, use case drift)
4. Feedback Loop Bias (reinforcement, selection bias)

### Detection Methods
- **Statistical:** Disparate Impact Analysis, Predictive Parity, Equal Opportunity, Calibration
- **Practical:** Slice analysis, diverse testing, adversarial testing, continuous monitoring
- **The Four Ws:** WHO is affected? WHAT outcomes differ? WHEN does bias occur? WHY does bias exist?

### Mitigation Strategies
- **Pre-Processing (Data-Level):** Data balancing, transformation, synthetic data generation
- **In-Processing (Algorithm-Level):** Constrained optimization, adversarial debiasing, fair representation learning
- **Post-Processing (Output-Level):** Threshold adjustment, outcome modification
- **Human Oversight:** Review and appeal, regular bias audits, external validation

## Transparency Hierarchy (5 Levels)

| Level | Question | Who Needs to Know |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| 1. Existence | Is AI being used? | All affected |
| 2. Input | What data is used? | Users, regulators |
| 3. Process | How are decisions made? | Technical reviewers |
| 4. Factors | What influences outcomes? | Decision subjects |
| 5. Outcome | Why this specific result? | Individual users |

## Explainability Techniques

| Technique | Best For | Limitations | Audience |
|-----------|----------|-------------|----------|
| Feature Importance | Understanding overall model behavior | Doesn't explain individual decisions | Data scientists, auditors |
| SHAP Values | Precise individual explanations | Computationally expensive | Technical teams, regulators |
| LIME | Quick local explanations | Approximation may be inaccurate | Developers, analysts |
| Counterfactuals | Actionable individual guidance | May suggest infeasible changes | Affected individuals, customer service |
| Rule Extraction | Human-readable model summary | May oversimplify | Business users, compliance |

## Trust Equation

**Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Transparency) / Self-Interest**

# UNIT 10: Performance Measurement and KPIs

## Core Idea
Design measurement framework BEFORE deployment, not after. Measure across THREE dimensions simultaneously. Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Numbers without narrative are noise.

## Unique AI Measurement Challenges

| Challenge | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Long Feedback Loops | AI benefits take months/years to materialise | Churn prediction model needs 12-18 months |
| Indirect Benefits | Value through second/third-order effects | Supply chain AI reduces waste -> improves margins -> funds innovation |
| Attribution Problems | Isolating AI's contribution from concurrent changes | Was 15% revenue increase from AI, new loyalty programme, or seasonal trends? |
| Changing Baselines | Environment shifts as AI deploys | Fraudsters adapt behaviour once detection system is live |
| Emergent Effects | Unexpected outcomes (positive and negative) | Scheduling AI improves efficiency but increases employee stress |

## The Three Dimensions of AI Measurement

1. **Business Outcomes:** Revenue, cost reduction, risk mitigation, strategic positioning — "Is this creating business value?"
2. **Technical Performance:** Accuracy, latency, reliability, scalability — "Is the AI system working properly?"
3. **Organisational Adoption:** Usage rates, user satisfaction, capability development — "Are people actually using it effectively?"

## Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

| Aspect | Leading Indicators | Lagging Indicators |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Definition | Predict future performance | Confirm past performance |
| Purpose | Enable early intervention | Validate outcomes |
| Examples | User adoption rate, data quality scores, training completion | Revenue impact, ROI achieved, cost savings realised |
| Limitation | Correlation may not mean causation | Too late to intervene |

## KPI Specification Template

Every KPI should be specified with: KPI Name, Definition, Dimension (Business/Technical/Organisational), Type (Leading/Lagging), Target, Red Threshold, Data Source, Frequency, Owner, Connected KPIs

## Targets and Thresholds — Traffic Light Model

| Zone | Meaning | Action |
|------|---------|--------|
| Green | Performance meets or exceeds target | Continue monitoring; share successes |
| Amber | Trending towards concern | Investigate root causes; prepare intervention |
| Red | Below acceptable threshold | Escalate immediately; activate remediation |

## Value Realisation Tracking

- Track projected vs. actual benefits for each initiative
- Track Time to Value (deployment to first measurable benefit)
- Watch for hidden costs: technical debt, opportunity cost, shadow workarounds, escalating cloud costs
- ROI Formula: **ROI = ((Total Benefits - Total Costs) / Total Costs) x 100**

## Dashboard Design Principles

1. **Right Audience = Right Metrics:** Separate views for executives, operational managers, technical teams
2. **Visual Hierarchy:** Most important information most prominent (top-left = most attention)
3. **Actionable Insights:** Every element should connect to a possible action

| Aspect | Executive Dashboard | Operational Dashboard |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Update Frequency | Monthly or quarterly | Daily or real-time |
| Level of Detail | High-level summaries, trends | Granular metrics, drill-down |
| Visual Style | Clean, minimal, trend-focused | Data-dense, interactive |

## Case Study: Starbucks' AI Measurement Journey (Deep Brew)

- **Digital Flywheel:** Self-reinforcing cycle of data -> AI -> personalisation -> engagement -> more data
- **Results:** Rewards members 17.6M->34.6M; 52% of sales digital; inventory automation, labour scheduling
- **Goodhart's Law in action:** Siren System measured speed/throughput but not customer experience — rollout caused operational disruption
- **Rewards discounting trap:** Membership grew but same-store sales worsened
- **Recalibration:** Shift from membership volume to engagement quality; from automating for efficiency to augmenting barista craft

**6 Key Lessons:**
1. Build data foundation before the dashboard
2. Measure across entire value chain, not just AI model
3. Beware metrics that become targets (Goodhart's Law)
4. Balance efficiency metrics with experience metrics
5. Expect to recalibrate — build that into your framework
6. Use public accountability as a discipline

# UNIT 11: Risk Management and Compliance

## Core Idea
AI introduces unique risk categories that traditional IT risk frameworks don't address. AI is dynamic (not static), probabilistic (not deterministic), black box (not transparent), cascading (not isolated). Documentation is infrastructure, not overhead.

## Five Core Risk Categories

1. **Technical Risks:** Model failures/inaccuracy, drift (concept drift, data drift), availability/scalability issues
2. **Security Risks:** Adversarial attacks, model theft/reverse engineering, data poisoning, privacy breaches
3. **Compliance Risks:** Regulatory violations, data protection breaches, sector-specific regulation non-compliance
4. **Reputational Risks:** Algorithmic bias, harmful/offensive outputs, privacy violations (2-5 year trust recovery; EUR 50-200M brand value loss)
5. **Strategic Risks:** Vendor lock-in (EUR 5-20M switching costs), technology obsolescence (18-24 month cycles), skills shortage (EUR 150K-300K per critical role replacement)

## AI Risk Assessment Framework (4 Phases)

1. **Risk Identification:** Brainstorming, historical analysis, scenario planning, expert consultation
2. **Risk Analysis:** Likelihood (1-5) x Impact (1-5 across financial, regulatory, reputational, operational, customer, safety dimensions)
3. **Risk Prioritisation:** Risk Score = Likelihood x Impact (range 1-25)
   - Critical (16-25): Immediate action, executive oversight, weekly reviews
   - High (10-15): Detailed mitigation plans, monthly reviews
   - Medium (5-9): Standard controls, quarterly reviews
   - Low (1-4): Monitor only, annual reviews
4. **Living Risk Register:** 13 fields per risk, continuous updating

## Risk Response Framework (4 Strategies)

| Strategy | When to Use | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| AVOID | Risk too high, insufficient controls | Don't deploy facial recognition in public spaces |
| REDUCE | Risk acceptable with proper mitigation | Add human review to automated credit decisions |
| TRANSFER | Risk can be shifted to third party | Cyber insurance, AI-as-a-Service vendor |
| ACCEPT | Risk low, mitigation cost > benefit | Accept low-level accuracy drift in non-critical system |

Each risk category requires **Preventive, Detective, and Corrective controls**.

## EU AI Act — High-Risk AI Requirements

1. Risk management system (continuous, ISO 31000 adaptation)
2. Data governance (representative, bias-free, quality-assured)
3. Technical documentation (minimum 10-year retention)
4. Record-keeping/logging (automatic, all operations)
5. Transparency and human oversight
6. Accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity
7. Conformity assessment before deployment (CE marking)

## UK Regulatory Approach — Five Cross-Sectoral Principles

1. Safety, Security, and Robustness
2. Transparency and Explainability
3. Fairness
4. Accountability and Governance
5. Contestability and Redress

## Monitoring and Audit Approach (Three Layers)

1. **Continuous Automated Monitoring:** 24/7 dashboards, real-time alerts, all high/critical risk systems
2. **Periodic Manual Reviews:** Monthly (high-risk), quarterly (medium-risk) by risk/compliance teams
3. **Third-Party Audits:** Annual for high-risk systems — technical validation, compliance, ethics

## Key Risk Indicators (KRIs) by Category

- **Technical:** Model accuracy %, prediction drift (PSI), data quality %, latency, availability
- **Security:** Failed login attempts, anomalous API queries, data access violations, adversarial inputs
- **Compliance:** Documentation completeness %, audit findings, regulatory changes, training completion %
- **Reputational:** Bias complaints, negative media mentions, fairness metrics, user trust surveys

## Case Study: HSBC's AI Compliance and Risk Management Framework

- **Scale:** 62 jurisdictions, 600+ AI use cases in production, 220,000 employees, 20,000+ developers using AI assistants
- **7 Principles for Ethical Use of Data and AI** (July 2024)
- **Three-Tier Governance Architecture:**
  - Tier 1: Global principles (universal, non-negotiable)
  - Tier 2: Regional compliance requirements (jurisdiction-specific)
  - Tier 3: Local implementation (culturally appropriate)
- **Operational results:** 60% reduction in false positive alerts, 2-4x improvement in detection accuracy, investigation time reduced from weeks to ~8 days

**10 Key Lessons:** Ethics first; governance enables innovation; centralise principles/decentralise execution; human oversight non-negotiable; documentation is infrastructure; extend governance to third parties; cross-functional capability needed; measure compliance; engage regulators as partners; build for evolution

# UNIT 12: Final Integration Workshop

## Core Idea
Individual strategy components are necessary but insufficient — they must work together as a coherent system. Common integration failure modes: siloed thinking, inconsistent assumptions, missing linkages, narrative fragmentation. The best AI strategy is worthless if you cannot communicate it effectively.

## The AI Implementation Strategy Flow

| WHY? (The Case) | WHAT? (The Approach) | HOW? (The Execution) | WHO? (The People) | HOW WELL? (The Measures) |
|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Readiness Assessment | Governance Framework | Technology Selection | Change Management | Performance Measurement |
| Value ID & Business Case | Ethics & Responsible AI | Data Strategy & Infrastructure | Workforce Transformation | Risk Management & Compliance |
| | | Implementation Roadmap | | |

## Five Integration Principles

| Principle | Description | Test Question |
|-----------|------------|---------------|
| 1. Strategic Alignment | Every component traces back to business case and objectives | Does each element clearly serve the stated business objectives? |
| 2. Logical Consistency | Timelines, budgets, resource assumptions, risk profiles consistent across all components | Are the numbers and dates the same everywhere they appear? |
| 3. Dependency Awareness | Plan explicitly acknowledges how components depend on one another | Have you mapped what must happen before each element can proceed? |
| 4. People Centrality | Change management, workforce transformation, ethics woven throughout — not bolted on | Does the strategy treat people as central rather than peripheral? |
| 5. Narrative Coherence | Strategy tells a compelling, logical story from problem to solution to value | Could a senior leader read this end-to-end and follow the logic? |

## Executive Presentation Best Practices

- **Structure:** Why -> What -> How -> What If -> Call to Action
- **Start with the "so what"** — Lead with impact and business value
- **Four credibility pillars:** Evidence-based, Honest about risks, Specific and actionable, Aware of constraints
- **Visual principles:** Simplify (one key message per visual), Quantify (specific numbers), Compare (before/after), Flow (timelines/roadmaps), Highlight (draw attention to key insight)

## Handling Executive Challenges

| Timeline Doubts | Reference comparable implementations; show critical path analysis |

- Business case <-> Readiness gaps, value opportunities <-> Implementation roadmap, KPIs, risk assessment
